# A MECE taxonomy of ML, AI, and data science as university coursework

**The full landscape of machine learning knowledge can be mapped to approximately 200+ graduate courses across eight top universities, organized into three pillars: Foundations, Frontiers, and Engineering.** No single existing resource provides this mapping — most roadmaps are either too shallow or too narrow. This taxonomy fills that gap, built specifically for a Columbia MSDS student with 6+ years of industry ML experience targeting Staff/Principal-level roles at frontier AI labs. The structure follows a Karpathy-inspired philosophy: build understanding bottom-up, implement from scratch, and progress systematically from fundamentals to frontier research. Each topic area below lists the best available courses with course numbers, professors, and affiliated research programs.

---

## The Karpathy method applied to a Staff/Principal trajectory

Andrej Karpathy's learning philosophy offers the ideal framework for structuring this taxonomy into a practical study plan. His core principle — **"you don't understand it until you've coded it from scratch"** — manifests in a progressive build chain: micrograd → makemore → minGPT → nanoGPT → minbpe → llm.c → microgpt (his February 2026 culmination: a complete LLM in 200 lines of pure Python, zero dependencies). His LLM101n curriculum for Eureka Labs lays out 17 chapters progressing from bigram models through transformers, optimization, distributed training, quantization, RLHF, and multimodal deployment.

For a Staff/Principal trajectory, this translates to three principles. First, **go deep before going wide** — master one research line completely (e.g., causal representation learning or model-based RL) before branching. Second, **foundations are non-negotiable** — Karpathy's "Recipe for Training Neural Networks" insists on becoming one with the data, verifying loss at initialization, and overfitting a single batch before any sophistication. Third, **systematic progression matters** — his series deliberately chose language models as the through-line because "most of what you learn will be immediately transferable." For each pillar below, courses are ordered from foundational to advanced within each topic.

No existing MECE taxonomy of ML knowledge exists in the literature. Daniel Bourke's 2020 roadmap, Daniel Formoso's mindmaps, the "Mathematics for Machine Learning" textbook (Deisenroth et al.), and numerous GitHub roadmaps all provide partial views. The gap is a rigorous, hierarchical, dependency-aware mapping connecting theory, implementation, and application across the full landscape — which this document aims to provide.

## Pillar 1: Foundations of ML/AI/DS

### Linear algebra, numerical methods, and matrix analysis

The mathematical backbone of nearly every ML algorithm. **Stanford EE 263** (Linear Dynamical Systems, Boyd/Lall) covers applied linear algebra with state estimation and optimal control applications. CMU **10-606/607** (Computational Foundations for ML) provides the matrix-oriented prerequisites specifically designed for ML students. At Columbia, **CSOR W4246** (Algorithms for Data Science) covers SVD, PCA, gradient descent, and SGD as core DSI material. NYU's **DS-GA 1014** (Optimization and Computational Linear Algebra) and Courant's **CSCI-GA 2420** (Numerical Methods I, Peherstorfer) handle numerical linear algebra, approximation theory, and iterative methods for sparse systems. For deep mathematical treatment, Columbia's **COMS 4995** (Mathematics of ML and Signal Recognition, Beigi) covers linear algebra, complex variable theory, measure theory, information theory, and linear ODEs/PDEs in a single integrated course.

### Probability, measure theory, and stochastic processes

Stanford **STATS 310A/B/C** (Theory of Probability) provides the full measure-theoretic treatment: probability spaces, law of large numbers, CLT, martingale theory, stochastic processes. Berkeley **EECS 126** (Probability and Random Processes) covers Markov chains, Poisson processes, Gaussian processes, and concentration inequalities. CMU **36-700** (Probability and Mathematical Statistics) and UIUC **STAT 551/552** (Theory of Probability I & II) offer rigorous alternatives. Columbia's **STAT GU4203/GR5203** (Probability Theory) and **IEOR E6711/6712** (Stochastic Modeling I & II, PhD level) cover through Brownian motion and stochastic calculus. UIUC's **ECE 534** (Random Processes) adds spectral analysis and Wiener/Kalman filtering.

### Bayesian methods and statistical inference

**David Blei's research program at Columbia** defines this space — his work on variational inference, topic models, and probabilistic ML directly informs Columbia's **COMS 6998** (ML with Probabilistic Programming) covering posterior inference, MCMC, variational inference, and Edward. Columbia **STAT GU4224/GR5224** (Bayesian Statistics) covers conjugate priors, MCMC, Gibbs sampling, and hierarchical models. Stanford **STAT 210A** (Will Fithian at Berkeley) and Berkeley's equivalent cover decision theory, exponential families, and sufficiency. CMU **10-624** (Bayesian Methods in ML) and **36-708** (Statistical Methods in ML, Aaditya Ramdas) provide rigorous nonparametric and distribution-free approaches. UT Austin's **SDS 384** (Bayesian Methods) and **SDS 386D** (Monte Carlo Methods) offer deep computational Bayesian training.

### Information theory and information geometry

Stanford **STATS 311/EE 377** (Information Theory and Statistics, Duchi) bridges entropy, KL divergence, and mutual information with minimax theory and hypothesis testing. Berkeley **EECS 229A** (Information Theory and Coding) covers channel capacity and rate-distortion. UIUC **ECE 563** (Information Theory) provides the Shannon-theoretic foundation. NYU's **CSCI-GA 3033** (Mathematics of Deep Learning, Joan Bruna) uniquely covers **information geometry and optimal transport** — Fisher-Rao metric, Wasserstein distances, and their connections to deep learning optimization. CMU **15-751** (Theory Toolkit, Ryan O'Donnell) touches information theory alongside Boolean functions and spectral graph theory.

### Optimization: convex, nonlinear, and stochastic

**Stephen Boyd's Stanford EE 364A/B** (Convex Optimization I & II) remains the gold standard, covering convex sets, duality, interior-point methods, subgradient methods, and decomposition. Berkeley **EE 227BT** (Convex Optimization, El Ghaoui) and **EE 227C** (Convex Optimization and Approximation, Hardt/Recht/Jiao) add modern connections to ML. CMU **10-725** (Optimization for ML, Yuanzhi Li) explicitly connects gradient descent, mirror descent, and accelerated methods to deep learning optimization including NTK and batch normalization theory. Columbia **IEOR E6613/6614** (Optimization I & II, Garud Iyengar) provides PhD-level convex and nonlinear optimization. UT Austin's **EE 381V** (Large Scale Optimization, Sanghavi/Caramanis) and **CS 388J** (Convex Optimization, Caramanis) are strong alternatives. UIUC **ECE 580** (Optimization by Vector Space Methods) adds functional analysis tools.

### Algorithms, complexity, and graph theory

Stanford **CS 221** (AI: Principles and Techniques, Percy Liang/Dorsa Sadigh) covers search, MDPs, game playing, and constraint satisfaction. CMU **15-850** (Advanced Algorithms, Anupam Gupta) and **15-780** (Graduate AI, Zico Kolter) provide depth. Columbia **COMS W4231** (Analysis of Algorithms) and **COMS W4203** (Graph Theory) cover isomorphism, planarity, colorings, and spectral methods. Berkeley **CS 270** (Combinatorial Algorithms, Satish Rao) and **CS 271** (Randomness and Computation) add randomized algorithms and LP relaxations. UT Austin **CS 388G** (Algorithms: Techniques and Theory) and **CS 390S** (Sublinear Algorithms) cover streaming and compressed sensing.

### Statistical learning theory: PAC, generalization, and complexity

This is critical for Staff/Principal candidates who must understand *why* methods work. Stanford **STATS 214/CS 229M** (ML Theory, Tengyu Ma) covers PAC learning, uniform convergence, neural network optimization landscapes, and overparameterization. Berkeley **CS C281B/STAT 241B** (Advanced Topics in Learning, Peter Bartlett) provides game-theoretic formulations and generalization bounds. NYU **CSCI-GA 2566** (Foundations of ML, Mehryar Mohri) — based on Mohri's textbook — covers PAC model, VC dimension, SVMs, and mistake bounds with exceptional rigor. CMU **10-716** (Advanced ML Theory, Larry Wasserman) covers nonparametric theory, minimax estimation, and function spaces (Hölder, Sobolev, RKHS). **CMU 36-725** (Statistical ML, Wasserman) and **10-726** (ML Theory: Art of Problem Formulation) add proof techniques. UIUC **ECE 543** (Statistical Learning Theory) covers ERM, Rademacher complexity, and minimax lower bounds. Columbia **COMS 4252** (Computational Learning Theory) and **COMS 4773** (ML Theory) provide the CS-department perspective. UMD **CMSC 742** (Algorithms in ML, Furong Huang) covers PAC learning with spectral methods.

### Probabilistic graphical models and state space models

Stanford **CS 228** (PGMs, Stefano Ermon) covers Bayesian networks, Markov random fields, exact/approximate inference, and structure learning. CMU **10-708** (PGMs, Eric Xing/Matt Gormley) extends into deep generative models, GNNs, and causal inference — a bridge course spanning Pillars 1 and 2. NYU **DS-GA 1005** (Inference and Representation, Joan Bruna) covers variational inference, MCMC, belief propagation, and structured prediction. Berkeley **CS C281A/STAT 241A** (Statistical Learning Theory, Michael Jordan) covers mixture models, HMMs, and state space models. Columbia **STAT GU4221/GR5221** (Time Series Analysis, Richard Davis) and NYU **DS-GA 1018** (Probabilistic Time Series) address state space models in the time series context.

### Control theory, optimal control, and dynamical systems

Critical for model-based RL, MPC, and Lyapunov stability research lines. UIUC has the most comprehensive pipeline: **ECE 515** (Control System Theory), **ECE 517** (Nonlinear & Adaptive Control), **ECE 528** (Analysis of Nonlinear Systems — Lyapunov stability, perturbation theory, input-to-state stability), **ECE 553** (Optimum Control — calculus of variations, maximum principle, LQG, differential games), **ECE 555** (Control of Stochastic Systems — dynamic programming, Kalman filtering), and **ECE 557** (Geometric Control Theory — differential geometry, Lie groups applied to control). Stanford **EE 263** covers state-space methods. Berkeley **CS 287** (Advanced Robotics, Pieter Abbeel) covers optimal control and trajectory optimization in the robotics context. NYU **CSCI-GA 3033** (Space Robotics, Riviere) covers linear/nonlinear dynamics, optimal control, and Bayesian estimation. UMD **ENEE 769L** (Decision Making Under Uncertainty, Kaiqing Zhang) bridges control with multi-agent RL.

### Decision theory, game theory, and causal inference foundations

Stanford **AA 228/CS 238** (Decision Making Under Uncertainty, Mykel Kochenderfer) covers MDPs, POMDPs, and multi-agent systems. CMU **10-734** (Autonomous Decision Making Under Uncertainty) and **10-880** (Game Theoretic Probability, Statistics and Learning) cover the game-theoretic foundations. Columbia **COMS W4775** (Causal Inference I, **Elias Bareinboim**) is the foundational Pearl-framework course: d-separation, do-calculus, backdoor/frontdoor criteria. Stanford **STATS 209** (Causal Inference) covers potential outcomes and DAGs. NYU **DS-GA 3001** (Causal Inference in ML, He He) offers an ML-oriented approach. CMU **10-708** includes causal inference within PGMs.

### Geometry, manifolds, and topological data analysis

NYU **CSCI-GA 3033** (Mathematics of Deep Learning, Joan Bruna) covers geometric stability priors, scattering transforms, GNNs, non-Euclidean geometry, and Hausdorff-Gromov distances. Columbia **COMS 4995** (Geometric Data Analysis, Andrew Blumberg) covers persistent homology, differential geometry, and optimal transport for data analysis. UIUC **ECE 557** (Geometric Control Theory) applies Riemannian geometry and algebraic topology to control. Stanford **CS 224W** (ML with Graphs, Jure Leskovec) covers graph neural networks and knowledge graphs. The **Geometric Deep Learning Course (GDL100)** by Michael Bronstein et al. (available free online) provides the theoretical framework.

## Pillar 2: Frontiers in AI/ML/DS

### Deep learning theory and architectures

Stanford **CS 230** (Deep Learning, Andrew Ng) and **CS 236** (Deep Generative Models, Stefano Ermon) provide breadth. CMU **11-785** (Intro to Deep Learning, Bhiksha Raj) and **10-707** (Advanced Deep Learning, Ruslan Salakhutdinov) span introductory to research-level depth. NYU **DS-GA 1008** (Deep Learning, **Yann LeCun** & Alfredo Canziani) is the flagship course covering architectures, EBMs, SSL, JEPAs, and contrastive learning — LeCun teaches his cutting-edge research directly. Berkeley **CS 182/282A** (Designing Deep Neural Networks) and UMD **CMSC 828W** (Foundations of Deep Learning, Soheil Feizi) focus on theory: optimization landscape, generalization, and implicit bias. Columbia **COMS 4776** (Neural Networks, Richard Zemel) covers architectures through generative models. UT Austin **CS 394D** (Deep Learning, Philipp Krähenbühl) combines theory with PyTorch practice.

### Generative models: VAEs, diffusions, flows, EBMs, and score-based models

Stanford **CS 236** (Deep Generative Models, Stefano Ermon/Aditya Grover) covers the full family: VAEs, GANs, autoregressive models, normalizing flows, EBMs, and diffusion/score-based models. Berkeley **CS 294-158** (Deep Unsupervised Learning, **Pieter Abbeel**) delivers state-of-the-art generative AI. CMU **10-799** (Diffusion Models & Flow Matching, Spring 2026) is a new dedicated course on score-based models, continuous normalizing flows, and flow matching. UIUC **CS 598** (Deep Generative Models, Arindam Banerjee) covers Boltzmann machines through diffusion models, and **ECE 598** (Generative Modeling with Diffusion and Flow Matching) is a dedicated offering. UT Austin **CS 395T** (Advances in Deep Generative Models, **Alex Dimakis**) covers foundation models, diffusion, and LLMs. For **diffusion on manifolds**, McGill/Mila's **COMP760** (Geometry and Generative Models, Joey Bose) is the only dedicated course covering Riemannian score-based models, normalizing flows on spheres/hyperbolic spaces, and differential geometry for generative modeling. Key researchers include Valentin De Bortoli (CNRS/ENS Paris), Emile Mathieu (Cambridge), and Yang Song (OpenAI). The **GRaM Workshop** at ICLR 2026 and ICML 2024 covers non-Euclidean generative models and flow matching.

### Causality, causal representation learning, and invariance

**Elias Bareinboim at Columbia is the definitive source.** Columbia **COMS W4775** (Causal Inference I) covers Pearl's Causal Hierarchy, do-calculus, and identification. **COMS 4995** (Causal Inference II) extends to transportability, causal RL, counterfactual reasoning, and causal representation learning. **COMS 4995** (Causal Trustworthy AI) applies causal reasoning to fairness. His textbook "Causal Artificial Intelligence" (causalai-book.net) provides a year-long curriculum with modular tracks: Causal Decision-Making & RL, Causal Generalization & Robustness, and Causal Generative Modeling & Representation Learning. His ICML tutorial on Causal RL is available at crl.causalai.net. Yoshua Bengio at Mila co-authored "The Causal-Neural Connection" (NeurIPS 2021) and leads research on interventional causal representation learning. **Bernhard Schölkopf** (MPI Tübingen) is a pioneer alongside Sara Magliacane (Amsterdam) and Caroline Uhler (MIT). The **Causal Representation Learning Workshop** has run at NeurIPS 2023 and 2024, and the **C♥LM Workshop** (Causality and Large Models) covers causality IN/FOR/WITH/OF large models. For IRM (Invariant Risk Minimization), the original Arjovsky/Bottou paper from Facebook AI (2019) is extensively discussed in these workshops.

### Reinforcement learning: model-free, model-based, hierarchical, multi-agent

Berkeley **CS 185/285** (Deep RL, **Sergey Levine**) is the premier course — Spring 2026, all lectures public. Dedicated lectures on model-based RL (Lectures 11-12), MPC, backpropagating into policies, and world models. Homework 4 implements model-based RL. Stanford **CS 234** (RL, Emma Brunskill) covers MDPs, policy gradients, bandits, and batch RL. **CS 224R** (Deep RL, **Chelsea Finn**) focuses on robotics applications: offline RL, meta-RL, sim-to-real transfer. CMU **10-703** (Deep RL & Control, Katerina Fragkiadaki) covers model-based/free RL, visual RL, and embodied agents. UIUC **CS 542** (Statistical RL, **Nan Jiang**) provides exceptional theoretical rigor: finite-sample analysis, function approximation, and off-policy evaluation. UT Austin **CS 394R** (RL, **Peter Stone**) includes multi-agent RL. Columbia **COMS 4995** (Theory of ML for Interactive Decision Making, Spring 2026, Adam Block) covers bandits, online learning, and RL from a theoretical perspective. **Shipra Agrawal** at Columbia (IEOR E4525) is the bandits/online learning expert. Berkeley **CS 294-190** (Abbeel/Russell) covers inverse RL, RLHF, and bounded optimality. For **model-based RL and MPC** specifically, Levine's course is primary, supplemented by MIT **6.8210** (Underactuated Robotics, Russ Tedrake) covering trajectory optimization and Lyapunov analysis, and Stanford **AA 203** (Optimal and Learning-Based Control). **Danijar Hafner's** DreamerV1/V2/V3 represents state-of-the-art world models for model-based RL.

### LLMs: theory, interpretability, alignment, and scaling

Stanford **CS 324** (Large Language Models, **Percy Liang**) covers foundations, scaling, alignment, and evaluation. **CS 336** (Language Modeling from Scratch, Liang/Hashimoto) builds a complete LLM including tokenizer, architecture, and GPU kernels — bridging Pillars 2 and 3. Berkeley **CS 294-267** (Understanding LLMs: Foundations and Safety, Dawn Song) and **CS 294-196** (LLM Agents) cover interpretability, alignment, risks, and agent applications. CMU **11-667** (Large Language Models: Methods and Applications) and **11-766** (LLM Applications, Fernando Diaz) span training to deployment. Columbia **COMS 6998** (Theoretical Foundations of LLMs, **Daniel Hsu**, Spring 2025) provides rigorous mathematical theory. NYU **CSCI-GA 3033** (Building LLM Reasoners, Greg Durrett, Spring 2026) covers FlashAttention, SFT, and RL for reasoning. UMD **CMSC 848R** (Language Model Interpretability, Fall 2025) and **CMSC 818I** (LLMs, Security, and Privacy) address safety dimensions.

For **LLM interpretability and alignment programs**: **MATS** (ML Alignment Theory Scholars) runs 12-week research phases in Berkeley with 98 scholars and 57 mentors including Neel Nanda (DeepMind) and researchers from ARC, MIRI, and Anthropic. **ARENA** (Alignment Research Engineer Accelerator) runs 4-5 week bootcamps in London — ARENA 8.0 runs May-June 2026 — with a freely available 5-chapter curriculum covering PyTorch fundamentals, mechanistic interpretability (SAEs, circuit analysis, superposition), RL through RLHF, LLM evaluations, and misalignment case studies. **AI Safety Camp** runs 14-week part-time online cohorts. **Redwood Research's MLAB** is a 3-week intensive in Berkeley. **CAMBRIA** offers 3-week bootcamps based on ARENA curriculum. The talent pipeline flows: AI Safety Fundamentals → ARENA/AISC → MATS → Lab positions. The **Mechanistic Interpretability Workshop** has run at ICML 2024 (140+ submissions) and NeurIPS 2025. Tel Aviv University offers a dedicated graduate seminar on LLM interpretability (Dr. Mor Geva), and Boston College's **MT875** (Mechanistic Interpretability, Eli Grigsby) covers geometry of neural networks and transformer circuits.

### Self-supervised learning, JEPAs, and energy-based models

**Yann LeCun's NYU DS-GA 1008** is the primary source — his 2022 paper "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence" introduced JEPA and H-JEPA, and V-JEPA 2 demonstrates it as a world model for robotics. His EBM tutorials are available through INCF TrainingSpace with multi-hour lecture series. NYU **DS-GA 3001** (Advanced Topics in Embodied Learning and Vision, **Mengye Ren**, Spring 2025/2026) covers 3D perception, self-supervised representation learning, continual learning, world models, and embodied AI — directly relevant to JEPAs. Stanford **CS 330** (Deep Multi-Task and Meta Learning, **Chelsea Finn**) covers unsupervised pre-training and continual learning. Berkeley **CS 294-158** (Deep Unsupervised Learning, Abbeel) covers SSL alongside generative models. For **system dynamics and Lyapunov stability**, the intersection with neural networks is an active research area: key papers include "Neural Lyapunov Control" (NeurIPS 2022) and "Safe Model-based RL with Stability Guarantees" (Berkenkamp et al., NeurIPS 2017). Key researchers include Sicun Gao (UCSD) and Russ Tedrake (MIT). UIUC **ECE 528** (Analysis of Nonlinear Systems) provides the mathematical foundations for Lyapunov methods.

### Continual learning and memory models

Columbia **COMS 6998** (Continual Learning and Memory Models, **Richard Zemel**) is a rare dedicated university course covering catastrophic forgetting, memory-augmented networks, and neuroscience-inspired approaches. NYU's **Mengye Ren** (DS-GA 3001, Embodied Learning) covers continual learning in the context of embodied agents and world models. Stanford **CS 330** (Chelsea Finn) includes continual learning within the meta-learning framework. **ContinualAI** (continualai.org) is the largest community: monthly seminars, the Avalanche PyTorch library (implementing EWC, SI, GEM, A-GEM, LwF, iCaRL), and a maintained paper repository. The **CLVision Workshop** runs at CVPR (6th edition at ICCV 2025), and the **Continual Learning Workshop** at NeurIPS has run since 2016.

### State space models: Mamba, S4, and cognitive modeling

**Albert Gu** (CMU Machine Learning Department, Goomba Lab) created the S4/HiPPO/Mamba lineage with **Tri Dao** (Princeton). No dedicated formal university course exists, but the topic appears in CMU **10-707** (Advanced Deep Learning) and is covered in UIUC's ML systems course (lecture slides available). The learning sequence follows the paper chain: HiPPO (NeurIPS 2020) → LSSL (NeurIPS 2021) → S4 (ICLR 2022) → S4D → Mamba (2023) → Mamba-2 (ICML 2024). "The Annotated S4" is the canonical hands-on tutorial. Gu's Goomba Lab blog provides the SSD (Mamba-2) deep dive. Notable hybrid architectures include Jamba (AI21), Codestral Mamba (Mistral AI), and IBM Granite 4.0. NYU **DS-GA 1018** (Probabilistic Time Series) and Columbia **STAT GU4221** (Time Series, Richard Davis) provide the sequential modeling context.

### Multimodal ML, vision-language-action, and embodied AI

CMU **11-777** (Advanced Multimodal ML, **Louis-Philippe Morency**) is the premier course: multimodal alignment, fusion, heterogeneous representation learning, attention, and temporal modeling. NYU **DS-GA 3001** (Embodied Learning, Mengye Ren) covers 3D perception, foundation model agents, and world models. Stanford **CS 231N** (Vision) and **CS 224N** (NLP, Chris Manning) provide the modality-specific foundations. Columbia **COMS 6998** (Deep Learning for Robotic Manipulation, Yunzhu Li, Spring 2026) covers foundation models for robotics. UMD **CMSC 848K** (Multimodal Foundation Models) covers vision-language pretraining and alignment. UT Austin **CS 395T** (Learning for 3D Vision, Huang/Pavlakos) and (Grounded NLP) cover vision-language grounding. Berkeley's "Robots That Learn" (Jitendra Malik) addresses embodied AI with sim-to-real transfer.

### Neuro-symbolic methods and OOD generalization

CMU **10-747** (Neuro-Symbolic AI) directly addresses neural-symbolic integration. CMU **10-732** (Robustness and Adaptation in Shifting Environments) covers OOD generalization and domain adaptation. UMD **CMSC 848P** (Theory of Robust ML, Fall 2025) provides adversarial robustness theory. Columbia **COMS 4995** (ML Security, Spring 2026, Suman Jana) covers adversarial attacks and model robustness. UMD **CMSC 828A** (Fantastic ML Paradigms) covers meta-learning, few-shot, and transfer learning. UT Austin **CS 389L** (Automated Logical Reasoning) connects SAT/SMT solvers to neuro-symbolic approaches.

### Safe, robust, and responsible AI

Stanford **CS 335** (FAT Deep Learning) and Berkeley **CS 294-139** (Fairness in ML, **Moritz Hardt**) cover fairness definitions and impossibility results. CMU **10-735** (Responsible AI) addresses safety, fairness, and accountability. UMD **CMSC 828Z** (Just Machine Learning, **Hal Daumé III**) covers algorithmic justice. NYU **DS-GA 1017** (Responsible Data Science) provides the data science perspective. Berkeley **CS 294-267** (Understanding LLMs: Foundations and Safety, Dawn Song) covers LLM-specific safety. CMU **17-445** (ML in Production, Christian Kästner) includes responsible AI within the deployment lifecycle.

### Port-Hamiltonian systems, physics-informed ML, and system dynamics

This is a specialized research frontier with no dedicated university course, but strong coverage exists in adjacent courses. The foundational paper is **"Hamiltonian Neural Networks"** (Sam Greydanus, NeurIPS 2019). Recent work includes "Port-metriplectic neural networks" (Cueto, Springer 2023), "Learnability of Linear Port-Hamiltonian Systems" (JMLR 2023), and "Stochastic Port-Hamiltonian Neural Networks" (arXiv 2025). Oxford offers a graduate course on **Physics-Informed Neural Networks** (PINNs), and Harvard IACS ran a PINNs course at ComputeFest 2024. The **NeurIPS "ML and Physical Sciences" Workshop** is the primary annual venue. Key researchers: Sam Greydanus, David Duvenaud (Toronto, Neural ODEs), Elías Cueto (Zaragoza), and George Em Karniadakis (Brown, PINNs pioneer). UIUC **ECE 528** (Nonlinear Systems) and **ECE 553** (Optimum Control) provide the dynamical systems theory. UMD **CMSC 838B** (Differentiable Programming, Ming Lin) covers differentiable physics simulations and neural ODEs.

## Pillar 3: Engineering and systems for AI/ML/DS

### GPU architecture, parallel computing, and hardware accelerators

Stanford **CS 217** (Hardware Accelerators for ML, **Kunle Olukotun**) covers custom accelerator architecture, dataflow, and hardware-software co-design. CMU **15-418/618** (Parallel Computer Architecture and Programming) covers multi-core CPUs, GPUs, and performance engineering. Berkeley **CS C267** (Applications of Parallel Computers) covers MPI, CUDA/OpenCL, and parallel algorithms. Columbia **COMS 6998** (High Performance ML, Dube/El-Maghraoui) specifically covers **CUDA programming, MPI, GPU architecture, cuDNN, parallel performance modeling, and HW/SW co-design** — the most directly relevant course for the user. UMD **CMSC 818J** (Domain Specific Architecture, Bahar Asgari) covers custom ML accelerators and domain-specific chips. UT Austin **CS 395T** (Prediction Mechanisms in Computer Architecture) covers ML-based hardware prediction. Stanford **CME 213** and UT Austin **CSE 392** cover parallel computing with CUDA.

### Deep learning systems, distributed training, and frameworks

CMU **10-714** (Deep Learning Systems, **Zico Kolter & Tianqi Chen**) is the gold standard: build a complete DL library from scratch including automatic differentiation, GPU operations, and efficient algorithms for convolution/recurrence/attention. Stanford **CS 336** (Language Modeling from Scratch, Percy Liang/Hashimoto) includes writing custom Triton kernels, memory-efficient distributed training, profiling, and data pipeline engineering. Stanford **CS 229S** (Systems for ML) covers efficient training/inference for LLMs, model parallelism, and quantization. Berkeley **CS 294-162** (ML Systems, **Ion Stoica**/Joseph Gonzalez) covers Horovod, Ray, TensorFlow/PyTorch internals, and hardware-software co-optimization. UIUC **CS 498** (ML System, Minjia Zhang) covers Transformer/diffuser performance optimization, compression, quantization, and framework design. UIUC **CS 598** (Systems for GenAI, **Fan Lai**) is cutting-edge: **PagedAttention, speculative decoding, pipeline parallelism, RLHF systems, AWQ quantization, vLLM, Orca, and distributed training** — reading from OSDI, SOSP, MLSys papers. UMD **CMSC 828G** (Systems for ML, Abhinav Bhatele) covers MPI, Triton, CUDA, PyTorch optimization using the university HPC cluster. UT Austin **CS 395T** (Advanced Topics in Systems and GenAI, Aditya Akella) covers scaling/serving/optimizing LLMs and diffusion workloads.

### Large-scale ML, data engineering, and MLOps

Stanford **CS 329S** (ML Systems Design, **Chip Huyen**) covers the full production ML lifecycle: data management, model deployment, monitoring, A/B testing, and MLOps. CMU **17-445/645** (ML in Production / AI Engineering, **Christian Kästner**) covers requirements, architecture, testing, deployment, monitoring, and responsible AI within DevOps. CMU **10-605/805** (ML with Large Datasets, William Cohen) covers streaming algorithms, MapReduce, and memory/communication complexity. Berkeley **CS 194-80** (Full Stack Deep Learning) covers end-to-end development including experiment tracking. Stanford **CS 246** (Mining Massive Data Sets, Jure Leskovec) covers MapReduce, LSH, and large-scale algorithms. Columbia **COMS W4121** (Computer Systems for Data Science) covers warehouse-scale computing, parallelism, and latency/throughput optimization. CMU **10-718** (ML in Practice) addresses real-world pipeline challenges. NYU **CSCI-GA 2820** (DevOps and Agile) covers CI/CD pipelines and Kubernetes applicable to MLOps.

### Efficient inference, quantization, and edge deployment

CMU **11-767** (On-Device ML) specifically covers edge deployment, model compression, and efficient inference on devices. Stanford **CS 336** includes quantization within its LLM training pipeline. Columbia **COMS 6998** (High Performance ML) covers quantization and model compression with cuDNN integration. UIUC **CS 598** (Systems for GenAI) covers AWQ quantization and speculative decoding. Karpathy's LLM101n curriculum dedicates three chapters to efficiency: "Need for Speed" I (CPU/GPU), II (fp16/bf16/fp8 precision), and III (DDP/ZeRO distributed training), plus dedicated inference chapters on kv-cache and quantization.

### Compilers for ML: XLA, TorchDynamo, TVM, and Triton

**Tianqi Chen** (CMU, co-instructor of 10-714) is the creator of **TVM** and a leading figure in ML compilers. Stanford **CS 336** teaches writing custom **Triton** kernels for LLM training. ## What the MECE taxonomy reveals about gaps in the landscape

Three structural observations emerge from mapping this taxonomy. First, **state space models and Port-Hamiltonian systems have no dedicated university courses** — learning remains paper-driven, creating an opportunity for whoever builds the first structured curriculum. Second, the **causality-to-foundation-models pipeline is concentrated at Columbia** through Bareinboim's lab — no other university offers a comparable two-course sequence connecting causal reasoning to modern ML at this depth. Third, **ML systems engineering is the fastest-growing pillar**: UIUC's Systems for GenAI (Fan Lai), CMU's DL Systems (Kolter/Chen), and Stanford's CS 336 (Liang/Hashimoto) all launched or were substantially updated in 2024-2026, reflecting industry demand for engineers who understand both the math and the metal. For a Staff/Principal candidate, the differentiator is precisely this ability to operate fluently across all three pillars — understanding why a method works (Pillar 1), what's at the frontier (Pillar 2), and how to make it run at scale (Pillar 3).
